I. Introduction

First and foremost, what are LLMs? LLMs or Large Language Models are AI systems capable of understanding and generating human language. LLMs are trained on a massive dataset of text and can be used in a variety of tasks, including ones related to journalistic work. The widely used LLMs include ChatGPT, Gemini, Llama, Claude, etc.

When talking about LLMs, prompts are also a crucial part as they provide the necessary context and instructions for LLMs to generate accurate and relevant responses. Well-crafted prompts can improve the quality and coherence of the output. Bad prompting makes it harder for an LLM to deliver useful, accurate, or tailored results. It usually happens when instructions are too vague, too broad, or too ambiguous. You must clearly tell the LLM the task, your audience, and tone. Do not assume it knows your goals.

How to Craft Your Prompts

  • Be specific and direct: Vague prompts get vague answers. Instead of saying ‚Äúhelp me write a pitch,‚Äù say ‚Äúwrite me a 100-word pitch to my editor about traffic lights in New York City.‚Äù
  • Provide context: LLMs perform better when they know the background. Keep reminding them of key facts throughout the conversation.
  • Define the format: Tell the LLM what format you want ‚Äî AP Style, active voice, JSON, bullet points, paragraph, etc.
  • Set constraints: Limit word count, ask it to avoid specific phrases, or restrict it to only use your materials.
  • Ask questions: Don‚Äôt hesitate to follow up or refine your prompt if the result isn‚Äôt right.
  • Use examples: If you want a specific tone, style, or structure, show an example.

Specific Steps

1. Determine the story you want to generate a follow-up idea for.
You need to determine the story first to set up the scope or areas of ideas you want LLMs to explore, and prevent responses that may be too general.

2. Upload the original story and ask LLMs to give you a summary of the story.
Asking LLMs to summarize the story is a way to test and make sure they actually read the original story and understand its context.

üß† Examples of Recommended vs. Weak Prompts

Recommended Prompts
  • ‚ÄúRead this article and summarize it:‚Äù [insert link]
  • ‚ÄúCould you fact-check this news article?‚Äù [insert link]
  • ‚ÄúPlease extract key points from this article:‚Äù [insert link]
  • ‚ÄúWhat are the main claims and evidence in this article?‚Äù [insert link]
Prompts to Avoid
  • ‚ÄúHere‚Äôs a link, tell me what you think‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúReact to this‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúWhat do you know about this story?‚Äù

3. Tell the LLMs about the context of the task you want them to perform. For example, you could input this prompt: ‚ÄúI‚Äôm looking for a hard-news angle to follow up on the story I‚Äôve just given you.‚Äù
Giving LLMs the context, like what types of angles you want them to come up with, could help narrow down the pool of ideas they would give you. Using the word ‚Äúnews‚Äù in the prompt would help LLMs understand that you want specific ideas that contain the newsworthy elements, not just general ideas.

üß† Examples of Recommended vs. Weak Prompts

Recommended Prompts
  • ‚ÄúCan you suggest some possible follow-up angles that could develop the story further?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúI want to pitch a follow-up for my newsroom ‚Äî ideally something with a fresh angle or unresolved question. Can you help me brainstorm ideas for a compelling next article?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúCan you help me come up with follow-up questions or angles ‚Äî maybe ones that point to deeper issues, new stakeholders, or consequences?‚Äù
Prompts to Avoid
  • ‚ÄúCan you give me follow-up ideas for this?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúCan you summarize and follow-up this and tell me more?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúGive me more about this.‚Äù

4. Ask for follow-up ideas from the perspective of a position on the news staff. For example, you could use a prompt like: ‚ÄúGive me follow-up ideas from the perspective of a sports editor.‚Äù
This kind of specification in the prompt will help LLMs be aware that they have authority over the topic they‚Äôre asked to provide. It helps their answers be more on-topic and on-theme.

üß† Examples of Recommended vs. Weak Prompts

Recommended Prompts
  • ‚ÄúI want the ideas to come from perspective of a sports editor ‚Äî meaning follow-ups should focus on sports-related angles, implications for athletes, leagues, or fan communities. Can you give me some ideas based on that?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúPretend you‚Äôre a sports editor at a major news outlet reading this article. What kind of follow-up stories would you assign to your reporters?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúCan you give me follow-up ideas from three different editorial perspectives ‚Äî a sports editor, a health reporter, and a data journalist?‚Äù
Prompts to Avoid
  • ‚ÄúCan you give me follow-up ideas for this story, maybe as a sports person?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúGive me some follow-up story ideas but like make them more sporty.‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúFrom the view of a sports editor, what do you think?‚Äù

5. Ask for creative or weird follow-up ideas.
Sometimes, LLMs‚Äô responses could get stuck in a rut of the same types of ideas that don‚Äôt illustrate much creativity. You could use this step to urge them to give different ideas that are more ambitious or provocative.

üß† Examples of Recommended vs. Weak Prompts

Recommended Prompts
  • ‚ÄúI want ideas that are creative and unexpected, but still realistic, newsworthy, and connected to the original story ‚Äî nothing too frivolous or speculative. Could you push the angles a bit while keeping them grounded in journalistic relevance?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúI‚Äôm looking for follow-up angles that go beyond the obvious, not just predictable next steps. They should still be appropriate for publication ‚Äî nothing gimmicky. Think along the lines of deeper context, overlooked consequences, or fresh reporting paths.‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúGive me 5 follow-up ideas based on this article. Make sure the ideas meet these criteria: (1) Not redundant with the original article. (2) Have potential sources or data to pursue. (3) Tap into underreported angles or gaps. (4) Can appeal to a general audience, not just specialists.‚Äù
Prompts to Avoid
  • ‚ÄúCan you give me some cool and interesting follow-up ideas?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúI want something wild, but also realistic.‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúWhat‚Äôs next for this story?‚Äù

6. Ask LLMs to explain why each idea it generates is relevant or good.
By asking the LLM to reflect on why its ideas are valuable, the user can get a better understanding of how the LLM generates ideas and using that knowledge, write better prompts in the future.

üß† Examples of Recommended vs. Weak Prompts

Recommended Prompts
  • ‚ÄúFor each story idea, explain why it would be a strong angle to pursue in terms of newsworthiness, timeliness, or audience interest.‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúFor each story idea, include a short rationale: what makes this a compelling, fresh, or urgent angle for a newsroom to pursue?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúFor each story idea, please explain why it works journalistically ‚Äî e.g., does it fill a reporting gap, expose an overlooked impact, offer a new perspective, or align with public interest?‚Äù
Prompts to Avoid
  • ‚ÄúCan you give me some follow-up ideas and tell me why?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúGive me ideas and comment on them.‚Äù

7. Narrow the list of ideas LLMs gave down to the ideas you‚Äôre mainly interested in, maybe one or two ideas.
This keeps the conversation simpler and lets the LLM hone in on specific ideas more deeply, rather than providing surface-level advice.

8. Ask LLMs for potential pitfalls for each idea you selected, or why they might be bad
AI is more easily self-reflective than most humans, which means it‚Äôs good at spotting places you could run into reporting errors or ethical problems in a story idea. Even asking for critiques on an idea you‚Äôve come up with yourself is helpful, as we‚Äôre less objective than LLMs.

üß† Examples of Recommended vs. Weak Prompts

Recommended Prompts
  • ‚ÄúFor each idea, please also explain any risks, challenges, or pitfalls that might come with pursuing it ‚Äî such as sourcing difficulty, legal sensitivity, or audience backlash.‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúCould you give me a few possible directions to pursue, and for each idea, note any potential drawbacks or concerns an editor or reporter should be aware of?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúFor each idea, briefly explain both (1) why it could work and (2) what might make it difficult or risky to report ‚Äî such as thin sourcing, limited public data, or political sensitivity.‚Äù
Prompts to Avoid
  • ‚ÄúCan you give me ideas and any problems?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúGive me story ideas and any reasons that maybe they won‚Äôt happen?‚Äù

9. Ask for the LLM to find similar stories on the internet to the ideas it proposed.
This will let the user find where the LLM got its idea, make sure the ideas aren‚Äôt redundant, and find inspiration for how to put the story together.

üß† Examples of Recommended vs. Weak Prompts

Recommended Prompts
  • ‚ÄúBased on the follow-up idea you gave me about [e.g., athlete mental health post-Olympics], can you search to see if this angle has already been covered recently by major outlets like ESPN, The Guardian, or the AP?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúFor each of the 3 follow-up ideas you suggested for this story, please search the web to see if there are similar stories already published ‚Äî especially in national or regional news sources.‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúHere are 4 follow-up story ideas I‚Äôm considering. Can you check if any of them have already been reported, and flag which ones feel overdone vs. still fresh based on what‚Äôs out there?‚Äù
Prompts to Avoid
  • ‚ÄúHave these stories been done before?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúAre there similar things out there?‚Äù

10. Ask LLMs to critique the pitch like an editor.
This is similar to the pitfalls, but adding the editor angle, as described in number four, gives you more pointed feedback.

üß† Examples of Recommended vs. Weak Prompts

Recommended Prompts
  • ‚ÄúFor each idea, explain the potential editorial or reputational risks ‚Äî from the perspective of an editor-in-chief who has to think about legal exposure, brand credibility, and public backlash?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúImagine you‚Äôre a standards & ethics editor reviewing possible follow-up ideas. For each idea, point out any red flags you would raise ‚Äî whether it‚Äôs about fairness, source integrity, or potential harm.‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúFrom the point of view of a section editor (e.g. sports, science, politics) responsible for both output and tone, what are some potential pitfalls with these kinds of stories ‚Äî including audience trust, editorial framing, or sourcing credibility?‚Äù
Prompts to Avoid
  • ‚ÄúWhat would someone in charge maybe not like about these stories?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúCan you tell me what could make editors nervous or worried?‚Äù

III. Scenario and Evaluation

We‚Äôre using Chat GPT and completing this from the perspective of reporters looking to follow up on this story: Washington Post: Easter Egg Alternatives

Here‚Äôs a link to our conversation, but we‚Äôll walk you through it: ChatGPT Conversation

1. When we uploaded a story about easter egg alternatives to ChatGPT, we wanted to make sure first that ChatGPT had actually read the article. Hence, we had it summarize the story, and as it could give us all the key points in this article accurately, we were sure that ChatGPT had read this article and was ready to proceed with other steps.

2. Next, we provided context that we were looking for entertaining angles to follow up on that story, and prompted ChatGPT to give follow-up story ideas from the perspective of a lifestyle editor. ChatGPT produced six ideas that were quite inapplicable.

Some of them seemed out of the scope of what we expected to be valid ideas or even sounded frivolous, such as inviting readers to share their ideas of unconventional dyed Easter eggs or what other household items that you can dye, while some of them seemed to be redundant to the issues that the original story had already covered, such as exploring why some alternative dyed eggs failed.

We inferred that words like ‚Äúentertaining‚Äù or ‚Äúlifestyle‚Äù are attributed to this sort of response. We may fix this issue by giving ChatGPT more context of what kind of use case of the ideas we are asking for, for example, tell it we want to use this idea to write a feature story for the newsroom.

3. As we had not gotten a usable idea yet, we told ChatGPT that the ideas it gave were redundant and asked for ones that were different from what was already in the article.

ChatGPT still gave a bunch of unapplicable suggestions, but also one we considered a good idea, looking at the history of Easter eggs and egg hunts, and why they‚Äôve stuck around. This is an idea that is plausible for a feature story for the actual newsroom, so we picked this one.

4. Then, we asked ChatGPT to reflect why the idea we selected was good or relevant. In its response, we somewhat disagreed with its rationale. However, it showed that the LLM was looking at it from an angle of entertainment, rather than news, as we framed it to be in the previous prompt, and that was a good thing to be aware of.

5. We also asked ChatGPT for the potential pitfalls it saw for covering this story from the perspective of an editor listening to the story. The concerns it posed were pretty solid and worth keeping in mind. It suggested that covering this angle could be too academic and dry, with no real takeaway to alienate readers by blurring religious lines in the piece. This could be an important consideration for someone planning to report on the story.

6. For the last prompt, we asked ChatGPT to search for similar stories that may cover this same idea and critique this pitch again, as it was an editor from a real newsroom.

This prompt confused the LLM and didn‚Äôt get what we wanted. However, it did provide more pointed concerns about the potential follow-up story from the vantage point of an editor.
